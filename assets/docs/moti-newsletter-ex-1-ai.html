Hello from the Mayor’s Office of Tech and Innovation!

This week, we explore the reality and limitations of AI and how it impacts everyday lives. Seemingly “smart” or “automatic” features like automatic email replies on Outlook or Gmail and suggested items to shop for on Amazon have become more and more commonplace across a range of tech products and platforms. But they aren’t always as automated/automatic as they might seem.

In fact, there is often a very real human element to these features hidden right underneath the surface. This week, we wanted to highlight a range of these because though we are definitely fans of technology and optimistic about its capacity for social benefits, at its core, technology is made by and for people, and ultimately impacts human lives, and we always want to keep that at the forefront of our mind, especially when working towards (re)designing more inclusive city services. Read on towards our Top Reads of the Week to learn more!

As a reminder, for our weekly digest, we strive to bring you relevant news you can use, at the intersection of civic life, government, and technology. You’ll also get to hear about an innovative project going on right here in San José with our Innovation Project Spotlight. And for our latest updates, don’t forget to follow our Twitter and Instagram.

Innovation Project Spotlight

This week, we wanted to highlight the visualization of Open Audit Recommendations through the City Auditor’s office. The City of San José conducts multiple audits of city services and city departments every year. The City Auditor’s Office regularly produces a report that catalogues the Status of Open Audit Recommendations that showed a backlog in addressing these recommendations. These recommendations are critical to improving operations for the City of San José to deliver services more efficiently and effectively to residents.

Photo:A screenshot from the City of San Jose's Open Recommendations as of June 30, 2018.

This project creates a digital performance dashboard to track progress against open audit recommendations and to estimate the cost to the city of not implementing these recommendations. The tool would significantly improve transparency and help the public and the City Council prioritize key recommendations from the City Auditor’s Office. Click on this link to get access: Open Audit Recommendations Dashboard. Thanks to the City Auditor’s Office for this great project and their commitment to public communication and transparency!

MOTI's Top Reads of the Week

Some companies use humans to train AI systems; others secretly depend on them while claiming to have scalable AI tech. Photograph: Westend61/Getty Images/Westend61

  • From The Guardian: The rise of pseudo-AI, how tech firms quietly use humans to do bots’ work
    • This piece from July of this year, explains the human side of “artificial intelligence” (AI) systems, both in terms of using human workers to train them and how some tech companies have used human workers secretly to give off the appearance of artificial intelligence. The article discusses not only the ethical implications of potentially deceiving users about what really is going on “behind the curtain” but also privacy implications from having personal data exposed as well as issues of labor rights for gig economy workers who usually complete the repetitive tasks used to train AI systems.

    • “I don’t like it,” said [Rochelle LaPlante, an Amazon Mechanical Turk worker and advocate for gig economy workers on Twitter] of companies that pretend to offer AI-powered services but actually employ humans. “It feels dishonest and deceptive to me, neither of which is something I’d want from a business I’m using.
      “And       on the worker side, it feels like we’re being pushed behind a curtain. I don’t like my labour being used by a company that will turn around and lie to their customers about what’s really happening.” [emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: This piece sheds important light on the importance of conscious design for tech interfaces/platforms. Designing accessible, easy-to-use services is important to us, but the human element underneath the surface that will be ultimately providing services at scale is also crucial.

Photo: A Facebook content moderator in Manila, one of the many hired by the Silicon Valley giant to monitor online content. (Courtesy Gebrueder Beetz Filmproduktion via The Mercury News)

  • From Gov Tech: Meet ‘The Cleaners’ who approve or reject Facebook posts
    • This great report from The Mercury News profiles a recently released documentary film “Cleaners”, about Facebook content moderators. It focuses on mostly young people working out of offices in Manila, and their struggles in this new type of job.

    • “What is the filmmakers’ hope for their documentary, which they filmed in the Philippines and elsewhere over the past few years? “‘We don’t want to be Facebook bashing,’ Riesewieck said. ‘It’s a huge dilemma,’ he added, involving the ‘public good.’ The filmmakers said they want to provoke open discussion about outsourcing content moderation to different parts of the world — and all that entails.” [emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: We liked how this piece highlights the global impact that tech platforms in our backyard have had. We understand the problems of one of the world’s biggest online platforms have likely never been encountered before. At the same time, we appreciate shedding light on this issue as a lot of political discourse and spaces continue to form in online platforms.

Photo Aygun Aliyeva / Shutterstock.com / Arsh Raziuddin / The Atlantic / GoogleMaps via The Atlantic

  • From The Atlantic: The Day I Drove for Amazon Flex
    • This narrative features a journalist's one-day experience using Amazon Flex, a gig-economy platform that allows users to work as ‘subcontractors’ for fulfilling Amazon orders.

    • “All my frustration really hit when I went to the second office building on Market Street, home to a few big tech companies. . . Young workers milled around with laptops and lattes, talking about weekend plans. They were benefiting from the technology boom, sharing in the prosperity that comes with a company’s rapid growth. Technology was making their jobs better—they worked in offices that provided free food and drinks, and they received good salaries, benefits, and stock options. They could click a button and use Amazon to get whatever they wanted delivered to their offices. . .
      “Until then, I had been, like them, blithely ordering things on Amazon so I wouldn’t have to wait in line at a store or go searching for a particular product. . .
      But now, technology was       enabling Amazon to hire me to deliver these packages with no benefits or perks. If one of these workers put the wrong address on the package, they would get a refund, while I was scurrying around trying to figure out what they meant when they listed their address as “fifth floor” and there was no fifth floor. How could these two different types of jobs exist in the same economy?” [emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: Through a day-in-the-life narrative, this piece is able to powerfully capture a friction that in some cases has accompanied rapid technology growth. On the one hand, making services easy-to-use and as hassle-free as possible can have amazing benefits; just look at projects by CodeForAmerica, GetCalFresh and ClearMyRecord that leveraged tech solutions, allowing more families to more easily sign up for CalFresh and for people seeking to clear their record to do so with relative ease. However, design can also obscure how the problem or service being delivered is actually being solved, and that disconnect can create very different economies living side-by-side.
